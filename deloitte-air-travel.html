Journey observations

Visibility only arrives one step at a time

Mapping the journey surfaced a pattern that matters more than any single pain point.

More touchpoints, more failure points

Six stages, six different apps and owners — every handoff is a new place something can go wrong.

It's a waterfall, not a map

You don't find out how long security will take until you're standing in it. Each step only reveals itself after the last one ends — there's no end-to-end visibility upfront.

So travelers constantly buffer

Extra time leaving the house, extra time at the gate, "just in case" — padding every estimate because they can't see what's ahead. That uncertainty is what's actually anxiety-inducing, more than any single delay.

Industry research

Across every source, the same three things surface

Secondary research — five independent source clusters, not just one survey — converges on the same pattern.

01

Delay & Cancellation Concerns

89% of travelers expect a delay or cancellation on their next trip; 2025 was the worst U.S. on-time performance since 2014.

Fortune / Hopper survey of 1,000+ U.S. travelers (May 2026); U.S. PIRG "Plane Truth 2026"

02

Post-Disruption Service

43% of disrupted travelers were still unresolved after 2+ hours. Top barriers: long wait times (48%), limited rebooking options (42%), not knowing what options existed (34%).

Fortune / Hopper survey, May 2026

03

Clear Communication

65% say communication matters most, but only 26% get timely updates and 39% get zero proactive notice during disruption.

Unily / Global Traveler, Jul 2026 — independently corroborates a UK CAA finding (~2/3 of disrupted travelers frustrated with communication)

Primary research

Real interviews confirm the pattern

Using a structured interview template — trip context, stage-by-stage stress, verbatim quotes, a synthesized opportunity signal — I talked to real travelers. Their stories independently converge with the industry data.

Sarah — leisure traveler, HSV↔MSN, basic economy, major delay + missed connections

  • Evidence: "~30 people missed the boarding time of this flight due to the security line" — after a 1+ hour TSA wait at 5:15am, her partner sprinted to the gate to make it.
  • Observation: only ~2 staff handled rebooking for ~50 disrupted passengers — capacity, not just communication, was the constraint.
  • Signal: supports the disruption-recovery hypothesis, and adds a new one — security-line and rebooking staffing capacity.

Arjun — leisure traveler, missed connection during disruption

  • Evidence: "Staff first said no alternatives were available — another agent eventually found a routing that got him home."
  • Observation: the outcome depended on which agent he reached; his airport parking reservation also expired mid-disruption, forcing an unplanned extension and extra charges.
  • Signal: independently confirms the recovery hypothesis, and reveals disruption's cascade into unrelated logistics.

Aparna — business traveler, HSV→DCA, basic economy, cancellation + secondary weather delay

  • Evidence: "The flight was delayed and then cancelled. I had to call customer support to find and confirm another flight." The replacement flight was then itself delayed by weather — a second disruption stacked on the first.
  • Observation: as a basic-economy passenger, she had no self-service rebooking path — recovery depended entirely on a phone call. The late arrival then forced her to adjust ground transportation on the fly.
  • Signal: confirms the recovery hypothesis, and adds two new ones — fare class gates access to self-service recovery, and disruption cascades past the airport into ground-transport coordination.

Insight: three independent interviews converge on the same root cause — loss of visibility and control once a disruption starts, not the delay itself. Aparna's experience adds a new dimension: recovery options are also gated by fare class, and disruption effects reach beyond the airport into ground-transport coordination.

Delay root cause

Most delay isn't weather — it's the airline's own operations

DOT's May 2026 breakdown of delay causes, by reporting carrier.

69%of delayed flights are airline-side
On-time 77.62%
Airline-side (carrier + late aircraft) 14.66%
External (NAS + weather + security) 6.50%
Cancelled / diverted 1.20%

Of all delayed flights, ~69% — roughly 7 in 10 — trace to airline-side causes, not weather (0.70%) or security (0.04%).

A nuance worth stating precisely: DOT defines Air Carrier Delay as explicitly "within the airline's control" (maintenance, crew). Late-Arriving Aircraft is a knock-on effect — a previous leg ran late and the same aircraft/crew carries it forward — not always the airline's "fault" in the moment, but a direct symptom of how tightly airlines schedule aircraft rotations.

So what: this reframes the opportunity — it isn't only about helping passengers after a disruption. A predictive layer that surfaces cascade risk before it fully hits (e.g., "your inbound aircraft is already 40 min late two legs upstream") could address the problem earlier in the chain.

Source: DOT Air Travel Consumer Report, Table 7B — Overall Causes of Delay by Reporting Operating Carrier, May 2026

Synthesis

When it's on time, it works. The moment it's disrupted, the same three things tend to break.

Across interviews and industry research, the highest-severity pain concentrated around disruption: travelers lose clarity, recovery options, and control over the rest of the journey.

1 · Generic communication is not enough

Airlines often do send delay or gate-change alerts, but they say what happened, not what it means for a traveler's specific trip — whether they'll make a connection, or whether to wait, rush, call, or rebook. Aparna wanted "clearer and more frequent status updates"; Sarah's gate info lagged reality.Only 26% of travelers get timely proactive updates during disruption (Unily/Global Traveler).

2 · Recovery capacity can't keep pace

Sarah: ~2 staff for ~50 disrupted passengers. Arjun: outcome depended on which agent he reached. Aparna: basic economy blocked self-service, forcing a phone call.43% of disrupted travelers are still unresolved after 2+ hours (Fortune/Hopper).

3 · The disruption leaks outside the airline

Arjun's parking reservation expired mid-disruption. Aparna had to rework ground transport. Sarah's connections unraveled.All three independently — none of these travelers ever needed the airline's help until the trigger hit.

Bridge to opportunity: DOT data suggests many delays are airline-side or cascade-related, not purely weather-driven — so the opportunity is not just better alerts after disruption, but earlier, more personalized recovery guidance.

Opportunity areas

Three opportunity areas emerge — in priority order

Each ties directly back to a synthesized theme and the evidence behind it.

Opportunity areaWhat breaks todayWhy this is worth prioritizing
1. Personalized disruption guidanceTravelers know a flight is delayed, but not whether they will make a connection, whether to wait, rush, call, or rebook.Strongest evidence across interviews and industry research; directly addresses panic, uncertainty, and agent overload.
2. Earlier risk detection & recovery triggeringAirlines often know operational risk before passengers experience the full disruption, but recovery starts too late.DOT data shows many delays are airline-side or cascade-related, meaning airlines may be able to surface risk and prepare options earlier.
3. Downstream coordinationFlight disruption spills into parking, rideshare, pickup, meetings, hotels, and arrival plans.Important adjacent pain point, but lighter evidence and more cross-provider complexity, so treat as a follow-on extension.

Recommendation: Start with personalized disruption guidance because it has the strongest evidence, highest traveler pain, and clearest path to a scalable product experience.
